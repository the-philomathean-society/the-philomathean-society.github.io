Philosophy
In debate, philosophy is a tool that can be implemented into your arguments. Knowledge of philosophical concepts can enhance and support your points, achieving a balance between practical and theoretical argumentation.
Benefits
  • Improve creativity of arguments: The ability to combine reasoning with insightful analogies demonstrates interpretation and delivery skills.
  • Improve reasoning and argumentative skills: Use concrete philosophical doctrines as evidentiary support.
  • Enhance persuasiveness: Strong philosophical evidence improves argument strength and audience appeal.
  • Improve external philosophical knowledge: Broaden your awareness and ability to apply theory in other academic contexts.
Common Philosophical Backings Used in Debate
Epictetus’ Theories of Stoicism
  • Man’s will is aligned with that of God; useful in arguments about the necessity of religion guiding human judgment.
Utilitarianism
  • Maximize pleasure, minimize pain
  • “Greatest good for the greatest number”
Deontology
  • Moral rules must always be followed
  • Focus on morality of action, not outcomes
Constitutionality
  • Decisions should align with the Constitution
Rule of Law
  • Follow laws or uphold the legitimacy of laws
Democracy
  • Voting system & democratic values like pluralism, access to info, active civil participation
Social Contract
  • Agreement between government and people; gives government legitimacy
  • John Locke: Inalienable rights; rebel if violated
  • Thomas Hobbes: People are violent; need Leviathan
  • Rousseau: Liberty through the general will
Sovereignty
  • States have full autonomy; morality is culture-specific (moral relativism)
Liberty / Autonomy
  • Maximize individual freedom; often tied to resisting oppression (e.g. racism, patriarchy)
Rawlsian Justice
  • Improve conditions for the least well-off
  • “Veil of Ignorance” ensures fair systems as we don’t know our role in society behind the veil
Equality
  • Egalitarianism: Equal treatment under the law
  • Equity: Treating people fairly ≠ treating everyone the same
Structural Violence
  • Social structures exclude some from moral consideration
Political Realism
  • States act only in self-interest; international realm is anarchic
Cosmopolitanism
  • All humans are in the same moral community; borders are arbitrary
Communitarianism
  • Morality comes from the community; we must preserve it
Virtue Ethics
  • Focus on developing virtues (e.g., humility, patience) to become a more moral person
